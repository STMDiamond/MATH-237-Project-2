Background:
Taken for two distinct high schools in Portugal, the Student Performance dataset from the UCI Machine Learning Repository measures student achievement. In other words, it focuses on the grades of 649 students for two different classes as well as their final grades when the school year ended. Additionally, it also includes other factors such as the education levels or occupations of their parents. Altogether, the dataset has 32 variables, which were all obtained either directly from the students' school grade reports or indirectly based on short surveys taken by them.

Research Questions:
After looking at the data, I was very interested in seeing how a student's daily traveling time to their school affects their own academic performance as their final grade. Then, I wanted to confirm if the time that a student takes to study a subject outside of class has any correlation with their own grade for said subject. More importantly, I would like to examine whether a student's number of fails or even a lack thereof contributes to their final grade. Lastly, I am curious to see how every student's number of absences affects their grades. As a result, I have decided to use this dataset to answer the following four research questions as listed:

  1. "Could the total amount of time that a student takes to travel from their house to school have any connection with their grade?"
  2. "Does the number of hours during which a student spends studying each week have an effect or impact on their grade overall?"
  3. "Is there a significant relationship between the total number of times that a student fails a class and their own final grade in the end?"
  4. "Would the number of times that a student misses school have any association with their grade?"

Reasoning of Research Questions:

  1. For the first question, I was wondering if students who travel long distances to their school would consequently perform poorly. Likewise, it may be the case that students who live closer to their school earn higher grades overall.
  2. As for the second question, I would imagine that students who study for longer periods of time earn better grades. Then, on the other hand, students who spend little to zero time studying would receive worse final grades or scores at the end.
  3. In the third question, I wanted to check whether my belief would be right or not. As an educated guess, I think that students who fail a class one or more times actually end up doing better than their peers who passed the class the first time.
  4. With the fourth question, I would just assume that students who have missed school more often than others have much lower final grades.

Part 5 Multiple Linear Regression

The features that I will focus on for $x$ are:

  1. The total time that a student takes getting to school each day in minutes.
  2. The total time that a student spends studying for class every week in hours.
  3. The total number of times that a student has failed a previous class.
  4. The total number of times that a student has missed a class or skipped school.

Then, the target that I will look at for $y$ is:

  1. The final grade that a student has earned on a scale from 10 to 20.

Putting this all together, the model for the multiple linear regression will look like:
Predicted Final Grade $ = b_0 + b_1($traveltime$) + b_2($studytime$) + b_3($failures$) + b_4($absences$)$
where $b_0$, $b_1$, $b_2$, $b_3$, along with $b_4$ are parameters in this model.

Part 6 Ethical Concerns and Data Limitations

First, an ethical concern that I have is if informed consent was present throughout the study, primarily while taking the participants' information. Although I can assume that the students gave their consent prior to filling out the questionnnaires, I cannot necessarily say the same for school reports since these do not need their permission. Speaking of the students' data, another concern would be that some variables like family relationships can contain sensitive details in nature. If this type of information was to be leaked in any way, it would most certainly be both damaging and devastating for the students from these two schools. Lastly, even other features which are not as sensitive such as the amount of free time that a student has after classes can cause harm in certain models. Moreover, this may lead to the false assumption that just having lots of free time can result in lower grades and worse performance.
Next, one data limitation is the lack of variety in both the high schools and subjects that were used for this dataset. Among all the countless private and public types of schools in Portugal, the data was only taken from two high schools. Even though these two high schools were not specified, I believe it is safe to say that they did not provide a proper representation of all schools throughout Portugal. Thus, this study could be improved by including a diverse and larger sample of schools from various neighborhoods or regions in the country. Similarly, the scope of this study was only designed with two fields in mind: Mathematics and Portugese language. It would have been nice to investigate other subjects including history, physical education, and science. Above all else, the ranges for some numeric variables were not consistent or equal with each other, most notably the pair between traveling times and studying times in this project. To add to this problem, some ranges such as the second one from 2 to 5 hours for studying times can be too general in their lengths because the actual time that was used to study could be as short as 2 hours or much longer as 4 hours. So, I believe that this study can be changed with other ranges that are not only narrower but also even such as less than 1 hour, 1 to 2 hours, and so on.
